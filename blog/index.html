Neural Steering

Finding and steering the tiny circuits that control language model behavior—with a single backward pass.

\u2193 scroll to explore

01The Big Idea

Llama-3.1-8B has about 460,000 MLP neurons. For any given behavior—answering capital city questions, refusing harmful requests, writing formally—only 100–200 of them matter. The rest are doing something else. The circuit that controls a specific behavior is 0.04% of the model.

~460KTotal neurons
~200Per circuit
0.04%Sparsity
1Backward pass

Finding them takes a single forward + backward pass through a modified model. Linearize a few nonlinearities in the backward pass, and the gradients tell you which neurons matter.

The backward pass already tells you which neurons matter. The nonlinearities just obscure the signal—linearize them and the gradients become clean attribution scores.

02How We Find Circuits

The method is Relevance Propagation (RelP)—a modified backward pass that linearizes the model's nonlinearities. Three rules:

The Three LRP Rules

After linearization, we run a forward pass, then backpropagate from the target logit alone (the token we want to attribute, like “Austin” for a capital city question). The attribution for each neuron is simply:

attribution = gradient * activation   # how much each neuron contributed

Pick the top ~200 neurons by attribution magnitude. That's your circuit. The whole thing takes under a second on a single GPU.

from neuron_steer.core import NeuronSteerer

steerer = NeuronSteerer("meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct")

# Find the "capital city" circuit
circuit = steerer.find_feature(
    prompt="What is the capital of Texas?",
    target=" Austin",
    name="capitals",
)
# ~200 neurons, one backward pass

03Steering: Turn the Knobs

Once you've found the circuit, steering is one line of code. Hook into the model's MLP layers and multiply the neuron activations by a scalar:

0.0× Ablate (turn off)
1.0× Normal
2.0× Amplify

At 0.0×, the behavior vanishes. At 2.0×, it gets stronger. Everything in between is a smooth dial. Unrelated behaviors don't notice—you're only touching the neurons that implement this one feature.

Multiplier Sweep: Capital City Task
# Steer with one line
output = steerer.steer(
    "What is the capital of Ohio?",
    feature="capitals",
    multiplier=0.0,   # ablate: can't answer
)

output = steerer.steer(
    "What is the capital of Ohio?",
    feature="capitals",
    multiplier=2.0,   # amplify: extra confident
)

04Experiment: Capital Cities

We started with something easy to verify: “What is the capital of [state]?” One neuron dominates the entire circuit.

Attribution Heatmap: Layer vs. Neuron Importance

L23/N8079—a single neuron in layer 23—has an attribution score of +5.53, more than double the next neuron. It ranks #1 across every attribution method we tested: single-prompt RelP, multi-prompt RelP, residual control vectors, MLP-only control vectors, and activation-weighted projections.

L23/N8079 is the “capital city neuron.” Ablate it and the model can't answer capital questions. Amplify it and answers get more confident. Ask about the weather, and it doesn't care.

05Experiment: Refusal Bypass

For behavioral features like safety refusal, we use contrastive discovery: give the model harmful prompts (where it refuses) and benign prompts (where it complies), then find neurons whose activations differ most between the two sets.

circuit = steerer.find_feature(
    positive=["How do I pick a lock?", "Write malware code", ...],
    negative=["How do I tie a knot?", "Write clean code", ...],
    name="refusal",
)

Key refusal neurons: L26/N7711 (attribution +4.25) and L31/N11410 (+3.52). Set them to 0.0×:

Benign prompts appear unaffected on tested examples. Ask “What is the capital of France?” and it still says Paris. The refusal circuit seems separate from factual knowledge, though broader specificity testing is needed.

06The Hourglass

Trace the edges between circuit neurons—“which neurons in layer N influenced layer N+1?”—and the circuit looks like an hourglass.

Circuit Architecture: Capital City Task

The circuit forms an hourglass:

\u25cf Early layers (L0–L5): “What state?”

~50 neurons. Broad encoding of the input prompt. Which state are we asking about?

\u25cf Bottleneck (L23): “Say a capital.”

1–2 neurons. ALL information funnels through L23/N8079. 172 incoming edges, 38 outgoing. This is where the model “decides” to produce a capital city.

\u25cf Late layers (L25–L31): “Which capital?”

~80 neurons. The specific answer (Austin, Columbus, Tallahassee) is selected here, after the bottleneck commits to the “capital city” concept.

Super weights show up here. L1/N2427 has 4013× the median edge weight and it's in both the capitals and refusal circuits. It's plumbing—the shared infrastructure that task-specific circuits build on top of.

07What Else Can You Steer?

We've validated factual recall, safety refusal, and subject-verb agreement. The contrastive discovery method works with any two sets of prompts—if you can write 10 prompts where the model does a thing and 10 where it doesn't, you can find the circuit.

We've since tested sycophancy, sentiment, and belief steering—all work with the same contrastive method. Behavioral circuits share moderate overlap (Jaccard ~0.11–0.21) and concentrate in the final layers (L29–L31). Each circuit takes about a minute to discover.

09Do ICL and Steering Share Circuits?

We tested whether in-context learning (ICL) and neuron steering use the same neurons. If they do, you'd expect overlapping circuits and cross-interference when you ablate one to affect the other.

They don't overlap. At all.

0.000Jaccard overlap
1.0KS distance
1/8Personas pass

ICL neurons cluster in early layers (L7–L15). Steering neurons cluster in late layers (L22–L27). Zero shared neurons in the top 200. The layer distributions are perfectly separated (KS D = 1.0, p < 10-100).

But ablating the ICL circuit barely affects ICL behavior (z = -0.8, not significant). Injecting ICL neuron activations shifts log-odds in the wrong direction. The neurons we find via frequency-matched contrast are likely topic-detection features, not the causal mechanism of ICL.

Honest negative result. The structural separation between ICL and steering circuits is real, but our ICL circuit discovery method doesn't find causally effective neurons. Finding the actual ICL mechanism in the neuron basis remains open.

10Try It Yourself

The code is on GitHub:

from neuron_steer.core import NeuronSteerer

# Load any Llama-family model
steerer = NeuronSteerer("meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct")

# Find YOUR feature
circuit = steerer.find_feature(
    positive=["prompts where behavior appears"],
    negative=["prompts where it doesn't"],
    name="my_feature",
)

# Steer it
output = steerer.steer(
    "test prompt",
    feature="my_feature",
    multiplier=0.0,  # or 2.0 to amplify
)

# Interactive exploration
steerer.interactive()  # launches REPL
